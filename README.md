# Quadruped-robot-design
Body and Chassis Shape The body is designed as a simple, lightweight rectangular plate with dimensions of 120mm x 60mm x 5mm. 
This shape is optimized to carry basic electronic components like a microcontroller and a battery without adding unnecessary weight.
Legs Design Each leg is designed with two main links: a thigh and a calf, standardized to dimensions of 10mm x 5mm x 45mm to resist bending moments. Hemispherical feet are added at the bottom of each calf to ensure point contact with the ground.
Number of Joints and Degrees of Freedom The robot features a total of 8 degrees of freedom. Each of the four legs has 2 degrees of freedom, provided by a shoulder joint for forward and backward swinging, and a knee joint for lifting the leg.
Motor Selection Micro servo motors, such as the SG90, are selected for this conceptual design. They are lightweight and provide the precise angular position control required to execute specific gait trajectories.
Initial Torque Calculation for One of the Joints The calculation is based on the worst-case scenario where the robot is supported by 3 legs, and the shoulder joint is fully extended horizontally. Assuming a total robot mass of 0.5 kg and a maximum horizontal leg extension of 0.09 m. The force acting on the supporting shoulder joint is F = m*g/3 , which equals 1.635 N. The maximum required torque is = F*d, which equals 0.147 N.m.
Stability and Center of Gravity The center of gravity is located precisely at the geometric center of the chassis. To maintain static stability, the design ensures that the projection of the center of gravity always falls within the triangular support polygon formed by the legs touching the ground.
Proposed Walking Method The proposed walking method is the creep gait. In this approach, the robot moves only one leg at a time while keeping the other three legs firmly on the ground, ensuring continuous static stability during locomotion.
Expected Mechanical Problems The first expected issue is foot slippage due to the low friction of 3D-printed plastic on smooth surfaces, which can be solved by adding rubber tips to the feet. The second issue is mechanical backlash between the motor gears and the leg links, which can be mitigated by tightly securing rigid servo horns with screws.
